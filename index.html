Why

My father taught me integrity: do what's right even when no one is looking. Schools gave me tools, but I've always been an independent thinker—capable of giving the expected answer when required, more interested in understanding how we got where we are and whether what we've built is actually helping or hurting. There was always more to unpack than the curriculum allowed.

Criminology showed me the range of human experience—not black and white morality, but shades of grey. Why people do what they do under pressure. Law school taught me how those dynamics get formalized into rules. Then I went inside: compliance at Capital One, regulatory work at a crypto exchange during a federal investigation, healthcare litigation, private equity. I wanted to see how the machinery actually runs.

The things people talk about most in private are the things I'm trying to address. Mental health, financial security, relationships that don't fall apart under stress, organizations that can explain their decisions and stand behind them. These aren't abstract problems to me.

I've always operated under a service-to-others model. That's who I am.
